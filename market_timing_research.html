The Real Cost of
Market Timing

What actually happens when investors sell during downturns and wait for "stability" before getting back in? We modeled five investor personas across 41 years of S&P 500 data to find out.

The conventional wisdom says "don't try to time the market" — and the usual proof is the "missing the best days" analysis showing how devastating it is to miss the market's biggest up days.

But that analysis has always been a straw man. Nobody actually misses only the best days. Real investors panic-sell during drawdowns, sit in cash, and then wait for the market to "stabilize" before re-entering. In doing so, they miss both the best and worst days. The question is: what's the net effect?

We modeled five investor personas — from the hair-trigger reactive seller to the disciplined rules-based timer to the buy-and-hold benchmark — across 41 years of S&P 500 daily data (1985-01-02 to 2026-03-04).

The Advisor Value Equation

For a disciplined buy-and-hold investor who would never panic-sell, the 1.15% fee could be perceived as a pure cost — For this client, the advisor needs to add value through other avenues, like planning, tax optimization, and estate work beyond investment management.

For the average investor who panics at a -15% drawdown, the behavioral coaching roughly covers the fee. Add dip-buying discipline ($2K at corrections, $4K at bear markets) and the advisor delivers +0.19% net alpha — turning $100K into $2.86M vs $2.66M.

For Nervous Nellie, the advisor's value is staggering: $2.24 million in additional wealth on a $100K portfolio, just by keeping her invested and putting $48,000 to work at the worst moments.

The Advisor Alpha Framework

The research above models what happens when investors try to time the market on their own. But the real question for advisors is: what specific behaviors drive the alpha you deliver? We identified seven sources of advisor alpha, ranked by impact.

Source #1: Risk-Aligned Allocation

The most powerful alpha is pre-emptive. Nervous Nellie doesn't exist if she's in a 40/60 or 50/50 portfolio. Cautious Charlie never panic-sells if his portfolio only drops 15% when the market drops 25%. The advisor eliminates the panic trigger before it happens.

The client who would have been Nervous Nellie in an all-equity portfolio may never feel nervous at all in a properly allocated one.

Source #2: Cost-Effective Fund Selection

Most self-directed investors end up in funds with expense ratios of 0.30–0.75%. An advisor selects institutional share class index funds at 0.03–0.10%. That 0.20% annual cost savings compounds dramatically over decades — nearly $400,000 on a $100K portfolio over 41 years.

Source #3: Behavioral Coaching During Drawdowns

Even with proper allocation, markets will test clients. The advisor's role is to be the voice of reason when every headline screams "sell." As modeled above, keeping a reactive investor invested generates +1.14% to +5.46% gross annual alpha depending on the client's temperament.

Source #4: Contribution Discipline

The single largest alpha source in our analysis: +1.08% annually from getting a client to commit to systematic $500/month contributions. The advisor who automates contributions so the decision is made once (not every month) captures this alpha permanently.

Source #5: Buy-the-Dip Framework

Contributing an extra $2K at -10% corrections and $4K at -20% bear markets adds +0.23% annual alpha. The psychological value is enormous: it transforms market drops from a source of fear into a source of opportunity.

Source #6: Rebalancing Discipline

When stocks crash and bonds hold, every human instinct says "sell stocks, buy bonds." The advisor does the opposite — systematic "buy low, sell high" that maintains risk alignment and circles back to Source #1.

Source #7: Goal Framing

Reframing "the market is down 20%" into "you're at 88% of your retirement goal" fundamentally changes the emotional response. This is the connective tissue that makes every other alpha source work.

Beyond Investment Alpha: The Planning Premium

Everything above focuses on investment behavior. But Steady Sam — our disciplined buy-and-hold benchmark — has his own problem: he probably works 2-3 years longer than he needs to, claims Social Security too early, and pays tens of thousands more in taxes than necessary.

Social Security Optimization

Most Americans claim Social Security early. Delaying to 70 means $80,400 more lifetime income assuming the client lives to 85. Live to 90 and the gap widens to $168,000. For a married couple, the optimization value can exceed $200,000+.

Roth Conversion Strategy

The years between retirement and RMDs (age 73) are a golden window for Roth conversions. Converting $60K/year at 22% during low-income gap years results in estimated ~$136,000 in lifetime tax savings versus letting the full balance face RMDs at 28-32%.

The 3 Years You Get Back

Steady Sam — disciplined, good saver, buy-and-hold investor — probably works until 67 "to be safe" when a comprehensive financial plan would show he could retire at 64. The advisor who runs a comprehensive plan and says "you can stop now" gives the client the most valuable gift of all: their time.

Additional Advisor Value
Emergency Fund = Anti-Forced-Selling Insurance

Without an emergency fund, an unexpected expense forces the client to sell investments — often at the worst possible time. The advisor who ensures 3-6 months of expenses sit in cash eliminates forced selling entirely.

Tax-Loss Harvesting During Drawdowns

The same corrections that trigger panic are tax-loss harvesting opportunities. Research estimates +0.20-0.50% annually. The advisor turns market pain into tax alpha.

Withdrawal Sequencing in Retirement

Which account to draw from in which year can add 0.5-1.0% in annual after-tax alpha. Combined with Roth conversion strategies, optimal withdrawal sequencing can save $100,000+ in taxes over a 25-year retirement.

Beneficiary & Estate Optimization

Proper beneficiary designations, Roth conversions before death, and trust structures for minor grandchildren — none of this shows up in investment returns, but it can preserve hundreds of thousands in generational wealth.
